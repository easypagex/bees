The Piece

A shrine for a sound
with no source

Kesh built the reliquary around an absence: the colony inside was never real. What remains is the architecture a hum would need to survive — layered velvet, gold seams, glass warmed to the colour of honey.

Reliquary of the Singing Bees is a freestanding cabinet piece, roughly the height of a chapel alcove, built from stacked bands of dyed velvet mounted behind hand-blown glass. Each stratum was cut and pressed separately, then sealed under brass moulding so the fabric reads as sediment rather than upholstery.

At the centre, a small brass aperture houses the piece's only kinetic element: a coil-driven resonator that produces a low, irregular hum when triggered, meant to suggest a colony working just out of sight. Kesh has described the sound not as recording but as residue — "what's left in a room after the bees have already gone."

The piece was assembled over fourteen months in Kesh's Lisbon studio and first shown as the anchor work of the Velvet Strata Interior series, a body of work concerned with domestic reliquaries for sounds that were never captured.

Category

Movement: Velvet
Strata Interior

A term Kesh coined for a small cluster of works built as interiors rather than objects — cabinets, cases, and alcoves upholstered in banded velvet, each layer standing in for a year, a room, or a held breath.

i.

The layer is the material

Every stratum of velvet is dyed and pressed as its own event. The banding is not pattern — it is a timeline you can touch.

ii.

Interiors, not objects

Works in the movement are built to be entered by the eye: cases, alcoves, and cabinets rather than freestanding sculpture.

iii.

Gold marks a threshold

Brass and gold seams appear only where one stratum ends and another begins — a visible edge between held moments.
